# Open Loop Mechanics — The Cliffhanger Playbook

Open loops are the engine of Soap Opera Sequences. Every email ends at a moment of tension. The reader must open the next email to get resolution. This file defines the five loop types, their World Code connections, and templates for writing them.

---

## The Cardinal Rule

**Every email ends with an open loop. Every email (except the first) opens by resolving the previous loop.**

The final email closes all remaining loops. No loose threads.

---

## The Five Loop Types

### 1. Culprit Tease

**World Code connection**: Conversation (Wrong Belief), Climax (Before State)

Hint at the enemy — the wrong belief, the broken system, the thing keeping them stuck — without fully naming it or explaining it. The reader feels the shadow of the problem but doesn't yet see its face.

**When to use**: Early in the sequence (emails 1-2). Sets up the antagonist.

**Templates**:

> But here's the thing nobody talks about. There's a reason [Before State behavior] keeps happening, and it's not what the experts say. Tomorrow I'll show you what's actually going on.

> I spent [time period] doing everything "right" and getting nowhere. Turns out there was something working against me the whole time. I'll tell you what it is in my next email.

> Everyone blames [surface-level cause]. That's not the real problem. The real problem is something you've probably never questioned. More on that tomorrow.

> There's a belief you picked up somewhere — maybe from a mentor, maybe from a course, maybe from watching everyone else — and it's the exact thing keeping you in [Before State]. I'll name it next time.

**Writing rules**:
- Name the category of the problem, not the specific problem
- Use "the real reason" or "what's actually going on" language
- Create a gap between what they think the problem is and what it actually is
- Don't be vague for vagueness' sake — give enough that they feel the weight

---

### 2. Method Preview

**World Code connection**: Method (phases, core principle)

Name a phase, principle, or step of your Method without explaining how it works. The reader knows the tool exists but doesn't have it yet. Curiosity pulls them forward.

**When to use**: Mid-sequence (emails 2-3). After the problem is established, before the full reveal.

**Templates**:

> I call the first phase [Phase Name]. It's the thing that makes everything else work. And it's probably the opposite of what you'd expect. I'll walk you through it tomorrow.

> There's a principle at the core of [Method Name] that changed how I think about [Climax topic]. It's simple. Almost too simple. But once you see it, you can't unsee it. I'll share it in my next email.

> When I finally figured out [Method Name], it came down to [number] phases. The [Xth] phase is the one everyone skips. It's also the one that matters most. More on that soon.

> [Method Name] works because of one thing most people overlook. I haven't named it yet. Tomorrow I will.

**Writing rules**:
- Name the thing (phase, principle, framework) — don't be coy about its existence
- Withhold the how, not the what
- Tease the counterintuitive nature if applicable
- "I'll walk you through it" is stronger than "Stay tuned"

---

### 3. Story Cliff

**World Code connection**: Voice (authenticity markers), Climax (transformation)

Stop a personal story at the moment of peak tension. The reader is inside the narrative and needs to know what happens next. This is the purest form of the cliffhanger.

**When to use**: Anywhere, but especially powerful in emails 1-3. The backstory and wall beats of SOS.

**Templates**:

> I was sitting in [specific place] staring at [specific detail] when I realized I had two choices. I could [option A — safe, Before State]. Or I could [option B — scary, unknown]. I'll tell you which one I picked tomorrow.

> That conversation changed everything. But not in the way you'd expect. It actually made things worse first. I'll tell you what happened in my next email.

> I'd been working on this for [time period] and I was ready to quit. Then [person/event] said something I couldn't unhear. I'll share what they said tomorrow.

> So there I was: [vivid scene of tension]. No plan. No safety net. And the one thing I thought I could count on had just fallen apart. What I did next became the foundation of everything I teach today. More on that tomorrow.

**Writing rules**:
- Set the scene with sensory detail before cutting away
- Stop at the moment of decision, not after it
- The reader should be able to picture the moment
- Don't artificially inflate the drama — real tension is enough

---

### 4. Contradiction

**World Code connection**: Conversation (Wrong Belief dismantling), Method (differentiation)

State something that seems to contradict what you just taught or what the reader believes. The cognitive dissonance demands resolution. They have to open the next email to reconcile the two ideas.

**When to use**: Mid-to-late sequence (emails 3-4). After you've established a principle, flip it.

**Templates**:

> Everything I just told you about [topic]? There's a scenario where the exact opposite is true. And if you're in that scenario right now, following my advice would make things worse. I'll explain tomorrow.

> Here's the part that confused me for years: [Core Principle] works. But only after you do something that seems to completely contradict it. I'll show you what I mean next time.

> I know I just said [previous teaching]. And I meant it. But there's a version of [Before State] where that advice is the worst thing you could hear. Tomorrow I'll tell you how to know which version you're in.

> The people who fail at [Method Name] aren't the ones who ignore it. They're the ones who follow it too perfectly. That probably doesn't make sense yet. It will tomorrow.

**Writing rules**:
- The contradiction must be real, not manufactured
- It should create genuine cognitive dissonance
- Promise resolution — don't just confuse them
- Works best when it challenges something they just started believing

---

### 5. Promise of Proof

**World Code connection**: Climax (Before → After transformation), Creation (offer results)

Tease specific evidence, results, or a transformation story without delivering it yet. The reader wants to see the proof that this actually works. Numbers, names, and specifics make this loop irresistible.

**When to use**: Late sequence (emails 3-5). When you need to move from teaching to believing.

**Templates**:

> I have a story about someone who went from [specific Before State detail] to [specific After State detail] in [timeframe]. Their situation was probably a lot like yours. I'll share it tomorrow.

> The results from [Method Phase X] surprised even me. One person [specific unexpected result]. Another [different specific result]. I'll break down exactly what they did in my next email.

> I could keep talking about why [Method Name] works. Or I could show you. Tomorrow I'll share the numbers from [specific context]. They speak for themselves.

> There's a [person/client/student] who almost didn't [take action]. They had every reason not to. What happened in the first [timeframe] changed their mind about everything. I'll introduce you tomorrow.

**Writing rules**:
- Be specific about what you're teasing (numbers, names, timeframes)
- The proof should match where the reader is in their belief journey
- Don't overpromise — the actual proof needs to deliver
- "I'll show you" is stronger than "I'll tell you"

---

## Loop Placement Rules

1. **One loop per email.** Never stack multiple open loops in the same closing. Pick the strongest one.
2. **Last 2-3 lines.** The loop lives at the very end of the email. Don't bury it in the middle.
3. **Resolve at the top of the next email.** First 2-3 sentences of the next email close the previous loop. Then open the new one at the end.
4. **Never resolve in the same email.** If you tease it, the payoff comes tomorrow. No exceptions.
5. **Final email closes all loops.** The last email in the sequence resolves everything. No loose threads. The reader should feel complete.

## Loop Sequence Patterns

### Origin Story Sequence (5 emails)
| Email | Loop Type | What It Teases |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| 1 | Culprit Tease | The real reason they're stuck |
| 2 | Story Cliff | Your lowest moment / the wall |
| 3 | Method Preview | The discovery / core principle |
| 4 | Promise of Proof | A transformation story |
| 5 | — (closes all) | Resolution + invitation |

### Method Reveal Sequence (4 emails)
| Email | Loop Type | What It Teases |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| 1 | Method Preview | The framework exists |
| 2 | Contradiction | The counterintuitive phase |
| 3 | Promise of Proof | Results from applying it |
| 4 | — (closes all) | Full picture + invitation |

### Creation Launch Sequence (5 emails)
| Email | Loop Type | What It Teases |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| 1 | Story Cliff | Why you built this thing |
| 2 | Culprit Tease | The problem it solves (deeper than surface) |
| 3 | Method Preview | How the creation works differently |
| 4 | Promise of Proof | Early results / transformation |
| 5 | — (closes all) | Full offer + closing the story |

## Common Mistakes

- **Too vague**: "Something amazing is coming tomorrow" — no tension, just hype
- **Too much revealed**: If you explain the thing, there's no reason to open the next email
- **Fake drama**: Manufacturing tension that doesn't exist in the real story
- **Stacking loops**: Opening multiple loops creates confusion, not curiosity
- **Forgetting to resolve**: An unresolved loop erodes trust faster than anything
- **Resolving too slowly**: The payoff should come in the first few lines of the next email, not buried halfway through
