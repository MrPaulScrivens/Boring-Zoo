# Discovery Question Patterns — Reference

Patterns for designing effective discovery questions in skills.

## The One Question Rule

Never batch questions. Ask one, wait, listen, build on the answer, then ask the next.

## Question Types

### Opener
Gets them talking. Low pressure, broad enough to start anywhere.
- "What do you teach people to do?"
- "Walk me through what happens when someone comes to you for help."
- "What's the thing you help people with?"

### Diagnostic
Reveals the real situation, not the surface one.
- "When someone comes to you for help with [their thing], what's the first question you ask them?"
- "What question do your students/clients keep asking over and over?"
- "What's the gap between what you teach and what people actually do with it?"

### Specificity Push
Moves from categories to situations. Use when an answer is vague.
- "Give me a specific example of that."
- "[Their word] could mean a few things. What does it look like in practice?"
- "When that happens, what exactly are they stuck on?"

### Signal Finder
Identifies what's already working.
- "What content/teaching moment actually got people to respond?"
- "Which part of your course/book do people quote back to you?"
- "If you could only teach one concept, which one gets the most 'aha' moments?"

### Belief Revealer
Surfaces their core conviction. Gold for output personalization.
- "If you could wave a magic wand and have your audience understand one thing, what would it be?"
- "What's a popular opinion in your space that you disagree with?"
- "What do most people get wrong about [their topic]?"

## Handling Vague Answers

When someone gives a broad/categorical answer:

1. Acknowledge what they said (don't dismiss it)
2. Ask for a specific situation or example
3. Use their example as context for the next question

**Vague:** "I help people with their mindset."
**Push:** "Mindset around what specifically? Money? Relationships? Career? What situation triggers the mindset problem you help with?"

**Vague:** "I teach marketing."
**Push:** "Marketing is huge. When someone hires you or buys your course, what specific marketing problem are they trying to solve?"

## Question Sequence Design

### Work Backward From Output
1. What does the output need to contain?
2. What information feeds each section?
3. What questions surface that information?
4. What order makes those questions feel like a conversation?

### The 3-5 Rule
Most skills need 3-5 discovery questions. Fewer than 3 and you don't have enough context. More than 5 and the person starts fatiguing.

If you need more than 5 questions, your skill might be trying to do too much. Split it.

### Build Context Progressively
- Question 1: Domain (what they do)
- Question 2: Method (how they do it)
- Question 3: Evidence (what's worked)
- Question 4: Gap (what's missing)
- Question 5: Belief (what they stand for)

Each answer gives Claude more context to ask a better next question.

## The Refine/Fresh/Keep Pattern

When a skill detects existing output:

**Refine:** Walk through each section of the existing output. Ask if anything has changed. Update only the sections that need it.

**Fresh:** Set aside the existing output completely. Run the full discovery sequence again. Produce new output.

**Keep:** Skip this skill entirely. Move to the next step in the sequence.

Always check for existing files BEFORE starting discovery questions. Respect previous work.
